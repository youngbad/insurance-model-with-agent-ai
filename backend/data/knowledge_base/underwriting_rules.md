# Insurance Underwriting Rules and Guidelines

## General Underwriting Principles

### Risk Assessment Framework
Insurance underwriting is the process of evaluating the risk associated with insuring a person or business and determining the appropriate premium to charge. Underwriters analyze various factors to assess the likelihood and magnitude of potential losses.

### Key Underwriting Factors
1. **Nature of the Business**: The type of industry and operations significantly impacts risk. High-hazard industries (construction, mining, chemical manufacturing) carry higher premiums than low-hazard industries (professional services, retail).

2. **Financial Stability**: Companies with strong financial health (high revenue, positive cash flow) are generally better risks as they maintain proper safety programs and can absorb minor losses.

3. **Loss History**: Past claims are the strongest predictor of future losses. A company with multiple large claims in recent years represents higher risk than one with clean loss history.

4. **Geographic Location**: Properties in areas prone to natural disasters (hurricanes, earthquakes, floods) require additional risk loading. Urban locations may face higher liability risks.

5. **Management Quality**: Companies with experienced, safety-conscious management teams tend to have fewer accidents and better risk management practices.

## Loss Ratio Guidelines

### Definition and Interpretation
Loss ratio = (Total Incurred Losses + Loss Adjustment Expenses) / Earned Premium × 100

- **Loss Ratio < 40%**: Excellent - very profitable for insurer
- **Loss Ratio 40-60%**: Good - acceptable profitability
- **Loss Ratio 60-75%**: Fair - marginal profitability, monitor closely
- **Loss Ratio 75-100%**: Poor - unprofitable when expenses added
- **Loss Ratio > 100%**: Unacceptable - insurer pays out more than received

### Industry Average Loss Ratios
- Property Insurance: 55-65%
- General Liability: 60-70%
- Workers Compensation: 65-75%
- Commercial Auto: 70-80%
- Professional Liability: 55-65%

## Claim Frequency Analysis

### Frequency Metrics
Claim frequency = Number of Claims / Exposure Units (employees, vehicles, revenue in millions)

### Frequency Benchmarks by Industry
- **Low Risk Industries** (Professional Services): 0.5-1.5 claims per 100 employees annually
- **Medium Risk Industries** (Retail, Hospitality): 1.5-3.0 claims per 100 employees annually
- **High Risk Industries** (Construction, Manufacturing): 3.0-8.0 claims per 100 employees annually
- **Very High Risk** (Mining, Chemical): 8.0+ claims per 100 employees annually

### Warning Indicators
- Claim frequency more than 2x industry average indicates poor safety culture
- Sudden spike in claims may indicate fraud investigation needed
- Concentration of claims in one location suggests site-specific hazard

## Premium Calculation Framework

### Base Premium Formula
Base Premium = (Expected Losses + Fixed Expenses + Variable Expenses + Profit Loading) / (1 - Variable Expense Ratio)

### Experience Rating Modification
Experience Modifier = (Actual Losses / Expected Losses) × Credibility Factor + (1 - Credibility Factor)

- Credibility increases with larger accounts and more years of data
- Modification factors typically range from 0.50 to 2.50
- A mod of 1.0 means average experience

### Schedule Rating Factors
Additional premium adjustments for:
- Superior management: -10% to -25%
- Poor management: +10% to +25%
- Special safety programs: -5% to -15%
- Unusual hazards: +10% to +50%
- Protection quality (fire, security): -5% to -20%

## Risk Categories and Loading Factors

### Property Risks
- **Fire Hazard**: High hazard occupancies (woodworking, chemical storage) add 15-30%
- **Natural Catastrophe**: Earthquake zones add 20-50%, Hurricane zones add 15-40%
- **Flood**: High flood zones add 25-60%
- **Crime**: Urban high-crime areas add 10-20%

### Liability Risks
- **Products Liability**: Manufacturing adds 10-30% depending on product type
- **Professional Liability**: Healthcare, legal, financial services carry significant exposure
- **Environmental Liability**: Chemical handling, waste management add 30-100%
- **Cyber Liability**: Data-heavy operations (tech, healthcare, finance) face growing exposure

### Employee-Related Risks
- **Workers Compensation**: Based on payroll × class code rate
- **Employment Practices**: Growing exposure for mid-size companies
- **Benefits Liability**: Plan administration errors

## Asset Valuation Standards

### Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value
- **Replacement Cost**: Full cost to replace with like kind and quality (no depreciation)
- **Actual Cash Value**: Replacement cost minus depreciation
- Most commercial policies use replacement cost to avoid coinsurance penalties

### Coinsurance Requirements
Most property policies require insuring to 80-90% of replacement cost value. Failure to meet coinsurance requirements results in penalty at time of loss.

Example: If building worth $1M is insured for $600K (60%) against 80% requirement:
Claim paid = (Amount of Insurance / Required Insurance) × Loss
= ($600K / $800K) × Loss = 75% of actual loss

## Operational Complexity Assessment

### Complexity Score Components (1-10 scale)
1. Number of distinct business operations (1-3 low, 4-6 medium, 7-10 high)
2. Geographic spread (single location=1, regional=4, national=7, international=10)
3. Supply chain complexity
4. Regulatory environment
5. Technology dependency
6. Workforce diversity and skills
7. Environmental sensitivity
8. Third-party dependency

### Complexity Score Impact on Premium
- Score 1-3: Standard rates apply
- Score 4-6: +5% to +15% loading
- Score 7-9: +15% to +35% loading
- Score 10: Special underwriting required, +35%+ loading
